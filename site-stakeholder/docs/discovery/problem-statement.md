# Problem Statement

A problem statement is not a symptom description. It is the precise articulation of the gap between the current state and the desired state — specific enough to evaluate potential solutions against, broad enough to remain valid as the project evolves.

The EDQMS project problem statement was produced by synthesising two inputs from the Discovery phase: the validated assumptions review and the opportunities map developed during the workshop.

## The Gap

The discovery work established a clear picture of the current state:

**Current state:** The Global Engineering Hub has engineering processes defined at a high level. However, these processes are not connected to the specific events that initiate them, nor to the detailed procedures that govern how each activity should be executed. When something happens in the operation, individual team members must rely on their own knowledge and informal communication to determine what to do and how to do it.

**Desired state:** Any team member — regardless of location, experience level, or familiarity with a specific case type — can respond to an operational event with confidence: knowing immediately what action is required, what the relevant requirements and constraints are, and how to execute the response according to a documented, auditable procedure.

The gap between these two states is the problem the EDQMS project is solving.

## The Problem Statement

> *"The Engineering Hub lacks a structured, event-driven execution architecture that connects operational events to the processes, activities, and procedures that govern the correct response — making it impossible to ensure consistent, auditable execution across distributed teams and regions."*

## The Three Dimensions of the Problem

The problem has three dimensions that any solution must address simultaneously:

### 1. Temporal: When to act
The system must be triggered by events — things that actually happen in the operation — not by calendar schedules or informal requests. A customer sends a request for an offer calculation. A technical finding requires analysis. An inspection scope is released. These events must activate a defined, traceable response.

### 2. Normative: What is required
Every response must be grounded in the requirements and constraints that apply to that specific event: the product type, the service scope, the customer's contractual obligations, and the applicable regulatory standards. "What is required" cannot be left to individual interpretation.

### 3. Procedural: How to execute it
Every action taken in response to an event must be executable by any qualified team member through a documented procedure — capturing the steps, the responsible roles, the required tools, and the expected outputs.

These three dimensions correspond directly to the project question:

> *"When something happens, will we know **when to act**, **what is required**, and **how to execute it all**?"*

## Why Standard Operating Procedures

The workshop produced an opportunities map covering dozens of specific improvement areas. After analysis, it became clear that most of them were symptoms of the same structural gap. Addressing them individually, one by one, would be expensive and would not produce a replicable system.

The decision made at the workshop was to address the root cause directly: build a template — a minimum viable data model — for creating and managing Standard Operating Procedures that are:

- Triggered by events
- Grounded in requirements and constraints
- Documented at the procedure level with traceable steps, roles, and outputs
- Aligned with ISO 9001:2015 so they can be integrated into the organisation's existing quality framework

This is the solution being prototyped in Phase 2 and operationalised in Phase 3.

## Connection to Business Value

The business case for solving this problem is anchored in two structural concerns that the discovery work surfaced directly from the Engineering Hub's operations.

**Onboarding continuity** — As the hub grows and new engineers and managers join distributed teams, there is no structured system they can reference to understand how work is executed, who is responsible, and what requirements apply. Knowledge lives with individuals, not with the organisation. Every new hire is therefore a risk to operational continuity rather than a capacity addition.

**Strategy resilience** — When internal or external variables change — organisational restructuring, shifts in customer requirements, regulatory updates, new regional scope — there is no documented baseline to evaluate what must be adapted and what can be preserved. The absence of that baseline means the operation must improvise its response every time.

Both concerns point to the same structural absence: a governance architecture capable of connecting operational events to the decisions, procedures, and requirements that govern the correct response.

Solving the problem defined above is what fills that absence. The three organisational outcomes that result are:

| Outcome | What it means in practice |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Onboarding speed** | New hires have a documented system to reference from day one — reducing dependence on informal knowledge transfer and individual availability |
| **Cross-regional consistency** | Any qualified engineer in any region can execute a procedure correctly, without relying on institutional memory held in a specific location |
| **Governance at scale** | As the hub grows, quality controls scale with it — embedded in the execution architecture rather than layered on top as periodic audits |
