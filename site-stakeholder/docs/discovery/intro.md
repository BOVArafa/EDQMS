# Discovery — Introduction

The Discovery phase was not designed to produce a solution. It was designed to produce the right question.

This distinction matters. When an organisation commences a new initiative — especially the launch of a new global engineering structure — the temptation is to move quickly from symptom to solution. The Discovery phase was deliberately structured to resist that temptation and to validate whether the problem being solved was actually the right problem.

## Why a Discovery Phase

The engagement began with a hypothesis from the Engineering Hub's leadership: the problem was one of capacity. The hub had projected staffing levels based on demand forecasts submitted by business units, and the question posed at the outset was:

> *"What needs to be developed or improved to prepare this engineering team to meet the existing demand forecast?"*

The Discovery process was designed to test that hypothesis — not to confirm it.

## How the Discovery Was Structured

The discovery methodology followed a structured, two-stage approach:

### Stage 1: Contextual Analysis

Before evaluating processes, it was necessary to understand the operating environment. This meant examining:

- The market context for power transformer repair across the relevant regions
- The existing Quality Management System and how it applied to the new hub's scope
- The risk and opportunity landscape that would shape process design

This stage was guided by ISO 9001:2015 §4.1 (organisational context), §4.2 (interested party requirements), and §4.3 (QMS scope). The standard provided the analytical framework precisely because it requires organisations to look outward before designing inward.

### Stage 2: Process Evaluation and Workshop

The second stage went inside the operations. It examined the existing offer process maps, the roles and competencies required, and — most critically — how the people doing the work understood what was expected of them.

This stage culminated in a workshop that brought together stakeholders and actors to map improvement opportunities. The workshop did not deliver a solution. It delivered something more valuable: a clear, shared understanding of the real problem.

## What the Discovery Revealed

The discovery process found that the hypothesis was partially right but had misidentified the root cause. The problem was not purely a capacity gap. It was a knowledge and execution architecture gap: the organisation had processes defined at a high level, but those processes were not connected to the specific events and decisions that triggered them, nor to the detailed procedures that governed execution.

The result was that team members — regardless of experience or seniority — could not reliably answer three questions when something happened in the operation:

1. Should we act now?
2. What exactly is required of us?
3. How do we execute it correctly?

The Discovery phase transformed these three questions into a single project question that became the strategic anchor for everything that followed.

## The Purpose of This Section

The pages in this Discovery section walk you through each element of the discovery work: the initial assumptions that shaped the engagement, the workshop methodology used to surface the real problem, and the problem statement that emerged from it. Together, they document not just what was found, but how it was found — so that the strategic choices made in subsequent phases are traceable to the evidence that justified them.
