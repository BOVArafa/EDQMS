# Assumptions

Every project starts with a set of assumptions. Naming them explicitly is what separates rigorous discovery from confirmation bias. This page documents the assumptions that Siemens Energy brought to the start of the engagement — the customer's view of the problem before the discovery work began — and how those assumptions were analysed.

## The Starting Hypothesis

The Engineering Hub's leadership entered the engagement with a clear view of the problem:

> *"What needs to be developed or improved to prepare this engineering team to meet the existing demand forecast?"*

This framing assumed that:

1. The demand forecast was reliable and well-founded
2. The problem was primarily one of **team readiness** — skills, roles, and capacity
3. The solution would be primarily **operational** — defining competencies and preparing the team to execute against known demand

These were reasonable assumptions for a newly formed engineering hub. They were also, as the discovery work revealed, incomplete.

## How the Assumptions Were Analysed

Two analytical tools were used to test the starting hypothesis:

### Business Model Mapping

The first step was to map the business model of the Engineering Hub together with its manager. This mapping revealed structural ambiguities that the demand forecast had not accounted for:

**The hub's business model** is based on business units providing annual forecasts of engineering hours, which they consume monthly through a fee arrangement. Engineers are distributed across regions and are expected to serve any business unit without preference.

**What the mapping found:** Business units had submitted demand forecasts without being able to explain the reasoning behind the projected hours. When the hub manager asked them to substantiate the numbers, the business units could not provide clear justification. The root cause was straightforward but consequential: **neither side had clarity on the service scopes the hub would deliver**.

### Case Type Scope Analysis

The second tool was a structured analysis of service scope types — examining what categories of engineering work the hub could reasonably be expected to offer, and matching those categories against the demand that business units had projected.

This analysis (documented in the Case Type Scopes spreadsheet) confirmed that the gap in demand substantiation was not a data quality problem — it was a **scope definition problem**. The hub had not yet established a shared understanding of its own service catalogue.

## Three Assumptions That Did Not Hold

By the end of the contextual analysis, three of the original assumptions had been challenged:

| Original Assumption | What the Analysis Found |
| :--- | :--- |
| The demand forecast is reliable | Business units could not substantiate their projected hours; the forecast lacked a clear scope basis |
| The problem is primarily about team readiness | The deeper problem was the absence of a defined service scope and clear operational procedures |
| The solution is primarily operational | A structural clarity problem needed to be solved first — scope, interfaces, and execution architecture — before operational improvements would have traction |

## Why This Matters

The value of surfacing these assumptions explicitly is that it reframes the return on investment. A solution that addresses only team readiness — training, hiring, capacity planning — without resolving the underlying execution clarity gap would have been expensive and largely ineffective.

The discovery work redirected the engagement toward the structural question: not "how do we prepare the team for demand?" but "how do we ensure that when something happens, anyone in the team knows what to do?" That shift in framing is what made the EDQMS approach both appropriate and necessary.
