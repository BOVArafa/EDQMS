# Role-Specific Recruitment Guidelines — Quality Manager

## Why This Role Is the Critical Hire

The discovery phase of the engineering hub's setup (PRP-C-0016) surfaced three operational gaps that, taken together, point to a single structural absence: **no one owns the connection between what the organisation does and why it does it.**

| Gap | What it revealed |
|---|---|
| **No substantiated demands** | Business units projected engineering hours with no documented rationale. Without a quality authority to challenge and trace those projections, commitments are made on assumption. |
| **Operational utopia** | Process maps reflected an idealised future state, not current reality. No one was responsible for maintaining the gap between documented procedures and actual execution — and closing it. |
| **Quality objectives disconnection** | Neither managers nor operational staff could explain how their daily activities connected to the organisation's requirements and objectives. This is not a communication problem; it is a governance problem. |

The Quality Manager is the role tasked with resolving all three gaps simultaneously. Without this hire, any quality system — EDQMS included — remains a design artifact rather than a functioning governance architecture.

### The Customer-Facing Consequence

Beyond internal governance, there is a strategic reason this role cannot be deferred: **how the organisation responds when something goes wrong is, for the customer, the most legible signal of whether the organisation takes quality seriously at all.**

When a process failure or product defect reaches a customer, the customer's immediate question is not technical — it is relational. They want to know: does this company understand what happened, and do they have a system for making sure it does not happen again? The presence of a Quality Manager answers both questions before they are asked. It signals that there is an accountable person, a structured process for investigation, and a formal mechanism for corrective action. The absence of one sends the opposite message: that the organisation has no designated owner for quality, which the customer will reasonably interpret as the organisation not caring about how its products are produced or delivered — and, by extension, not caring about solving the problem either.

In the context of power transformer repair, where failures carry significant financial, operational, and safety consequences, that signal is not recoverable through goodwill alone. A customer who experiences a failure and finds no quality structure on the other side does not distinguish between a process gap and an attitude gap. To them, the two are the same thing.

## The Role in the EDQMS Architecture

The EDQMS is built around the principle that quality management should be **simultaneously reactive and proactive** — driven by Events occurring within business operations, not by periodic audits alone. The Quality Manager is the human counterpart of that principle. They do not audit the system from the outside; they operate it from the inside.

In the EDQMS data model, the Quality Manager holds the **Broker role**: the authority responsible for creating Payloads — the business rules that wire operational Events to the correct Processes and Activities. This is not a passive documentation function. It requires understanding the full operations chain well enough to decide, for any given event, what process applies, what requirements are in scope, and how execution should proceed.

### Responsibilities by PDCA Phase

The PDCA cycle provides the clearest map of what this role does and when:

#### Plan — Build the governance baseline

- Define and maintain the Risk and Opportunity register, assigning Risk Priority Numbers based on Severity × Likelihood (ISO §6.1.1)
- Map Sources (internal and external), Requirements (customer, statutory, regulatory), and Constraints that bound the hub's operations
- Document Payloads: the business rules that specify which Activity or Process is triggered by which Event under which conditions
- Ensure each Process, Activity, and Operation has a defined Owner — eliminating accountability gaps across the operations chain

#### Do — Enable correct execution

- Author and maintain Procedures and their constituent Operations so that any qualified engineer in any region can execute them without relying on informal knowledge held by a specific individual
- Define Role competency profiles (education, training, skills, experience per ISO §7.2) to support targeted hiring and onboarding
- Translate quality objectives into operational terms that front-line staff can understand and trace to their daily activities — directly addressing the disconnection gap identified in discovery

#### Check — Monitor through Events

- Operate the Event monitoring layer: classify Events as they occur, validate that the correct Payload was triggered, and confirm that execution conformed to the documented Procedure
- Maintain Quality Gates at critical handover points within the offer workflow
- Report on process performance against defined metrics and quality objectives (ISO §9.1)

#### Act — Close the improvement loop

- Lead non-conformance handling: root cause analysis, corrective action planning, and verification of effectiveness (ISO §10.2)
- Update Risk Priority Numbers when Event data changes the severity or likelihood assessment of a known risk
- Revise Payloads, Procedures, and Operations when post-event analysis reveals that documented execution guidance was insufficient or incorrect
- Propose systemic improvements when recurring Events point to structural process deficiencies

## Competency Requirements

In alignment with ISO 9001:2015 §7.2, competency is defined as a verifiable combination of education, training, skills, and experience. The following requirements reflect both the standard's definition and the specific operational context of the engineering hub.

### Education

- Bachelor's degree or higher in Engineering, Operations Management, or a related technical field
- Formal qualification in Quality Management Systems (e.g., CQE, IRCA-certified Lead Auditor, or equivalent) is strongly preferred

### Training

- ISO 9001:2015 internal or lead auditor certification
- Demonstrated training in root cause analysis methodologies (e.g., 8D, Ishikawa, 5-Why)
- Familiarity with risk management frameworks aligned to ISO 31000 or equivalent

### Skills

- **Systemic thinking** — ability to model how process decisions propagate across the operations chain; comfort with entity-relationship thinking (not just linear flowcharts)
- **Written clarity** — capability to document procedures and operations at a level of precision that enables execution by a reader with no prior context, in a second language if required
- **Structured analysis** — ability to distinguish between symptoms and root causes; resistance to accepting "operational utopia" (idealised process maps) as a substitute for documented current-state procedures
- **Cross-functional communication** — ability to translate between the technical language of engineering and the governance language of quality standards without losing precision in either direction
- **Prioritisation under ambiguity** — capable of triaging competing quality demands when not all can be addressed simultaneously, using Risk Priority Number logic to justify sequencing

### Experience

- Minimum 5 years of experience in a quality management or process governance role
- Direct experience with ISO 9001 implementation or certification — not merely auditing an established system, but building or rebuilding one in an operational context
- Experience in industrial services, engineering-to-order, or heavy equipment industries is strongly preferred; power transformer repair or comparable capital equipment is an advantage
- Experience operating across distributed or multi-regional teams is required — the hub's governance mandate is global, not local

## Evaluation Criteria for Candidate Assessment

The following questions should be used to probe whether a candidate has the specific competencies required, beyond formal qualifications:

1. **Describe a situation where you inherited a set of process documents and found they did not reflect how work was actually being executed. What did you do?**
   *(Assesses: gap between idealised and real-state documentation; operational realism)*

2. **How have you ensured that front-line staff understood how their daily activities connected to the organisation's quality objectives?**
   *(Assesses: ability to resolve the quality objectives disconnection gap)*

3. **Give an example of a risk you identified that others had not substantiated. How did you build the evidence base for it?**
   *(Assesses: risk identification rigour; ability to challenge undocumented assumptions)*

4. **Describe how you have managed process documentation for a team distributed across multiple regions or time zones.**
   *(Assesses: cross-regional governance experience)*

5. **How do you decide which nonconformities to escalate and which to resolve at the operational level?**
   *(Assesses: prioritisation logic; RPN or equivalent threshold thinking)*

## What Success Looks Like at 90 Days

A Quality Manager who joins the hub and performs this role effectively will produce the following outcomes within the first 90 days:

- A current-state assessment of every process map in the hub's documentation library, with an explicit gap analysis distinguishing what is documented from what is actually executed
- At least one complete Payload defined in the EDQMS system: an Event identified, the correct Activity or Process wired to it, the governing Requirements and Constraints mapped, and the Procedure verified as executable by a new hire with no prior context
- A Risk register with substantiated RPN scores for at least the top five risks identified in the discovery phase — with documented evidence of how each score was derived

These three outputs operationalise the project question:

> *"When something happens, will we know when to act, what is required, and how to execute it all?"*

The Quality Manager is the person who makes the answer to that question "yes."
